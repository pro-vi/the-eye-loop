Here’s the cleanest way to see the Eye Loop:

It is not primarily an image generator, a recommender, or a UI tool. It is a **sequential experiment** on a hidden personal utility function. The system proposes visual probes, the user answers with a tiny bit of evidence—accept/reject, or better/worse, plus latency—and the model updates a posterior over “what kind of thing this person is actually moving toward.” In other words, Akinator asks symbolic yes/no questions; Eye Loop asks **visual** ones. That lands you squarely in the overlap of Bayesian experimental design, active preference learning, preferential Bayesian optimization, psychophysics, and interactive evolutionary search. ([ResearchGate][1])

The seven threads you listed really do collapse onto one mechanism:

1. a latent space of possible artifacts,
2. a hidden user-specific utility function over that space,
3. a query policy that chooses the next artifact or pair of artifacts to maximize information gain or expected improvement,
4. a noisy human observation channel made of binary choice plus reaction time,
5. a proposal mechanism that explores locally and globally,
6. a memory of attractive and repulsive regions, and
7. a representation that lets the model generate, perceive, compare, and critique in one loop. ([NeurIPS Papers][2])

## 1) Information-theoretic preference elicitation: this is the mathematical core

The formal umbrella is **Bayesian optimal experimental design**: choose the next query that maximizes expected reduction in uncertainty about the latent preference parameters, often framed as expected information gain or mutual information. In active learning, the best-known practical approximations include **uncertainty sampling** (query the point the model is least certain about), **query-by-committee** (query where plausible models disagree most), and **BALD**-style criteria (query whose outcome would tell you the most about the model parameters). In choice/preference settings, the same logic appears in **Bayesian adaptive choice experiments**, **active reward modeling**, **Gaussian-process preference learning**, and **preferential Bayesian optimization**. ([Burr Settles][3])

So yes: Akinator’s “split the possibility space as evenly as possible” intuition is the right ancestor. The general form is basically **generalized binary search / noisy 20 questions**. The difference is that your “question” is no longer linguistic or symbolic. It is a generated stimulus whose answer depends on an unobserved aesthetic latent variable. That makes Eye Loop much closer to **active preference learning** or **dueling-bandit / pairwise comparison** problems than to classic chat-style preference collection. ([People at Cornell][4])

On the “minimum number of binary decisions” question: there is no universal magic number, because the answer depends on the hypothesis class, the noise model, and the precision you want. But there are solid guide rails. If the target lies in a **discrete set of N possibilities**, any noiseless binary procedure needs at least about **log2 N** bits. For ranking **m** items, adaptive pairwise elicitation has worst-case order **m log m**, though informative priors can cut average-case complexity substantially. For **continuous d-dimensional preferences**, favorable active-learning results can approach something like **d log(1/ε)** comparisons to reach ε-scale localization, while generic high-dimensional estimation without structural assumptions scales at least linearly with dimension; sparsity assumptions can reduce this to something closer to **k log d**. Noise makes all of this worse, sometimes sharply. ([NeurIPS Papers][5])

That gives a practical design rule: Eye Loop should assume it can converge quickly only if it exploits **structure**. Useful structure includes sparse latent factors, hierarchical aesthetic axes, good priors, or a generator latent space where local moves correspond to meaningful perceptual changes. Without that, binary feedback in a high-dimensional space becomes a very expensive hobby. ([arXiv][6])

## 2) Psychophysics and aesthetics: this is the observer model and the noise floor

Psychophysics gives you the right humility. Human discrimination is not a crisp oracle. Modern threshold theory treats discrimination limits as **soft and noisy**, not a hard just-noticeable cliff. In two-alternative forced-choice tasks, larger stimulus differences tend to produce faster and more accurate responses, while items near the decision boundary produce hesitation. Drift-diffusion-style models explain this naturally: evidence accumulates until one option wins, so harder comparisons take longer. ([PMC][7])

That matters because your latency channel is not just decorative telemetry. Recent work on **preference learning with response time** shows RT can improve reward/preference elicitation when modeled jointly with choice, and psychophysical work similarly shows that combining choices with RT can recover observer parameters more efficiently than either alone. The crucial subtlety is this: **slow** does not automatically mean “dislike.” It often means “these options sit near the user’s current indifference boundary” or “the evidence was weak.” In Eye Loop, hesitation is best treated as a measure of **boundary proximity / uncertainty / low margin**, not simply low preference. ([arXiv][8])

On empirical aesthetics, the literature is surprisingly compressible. In interface/web aesthetics, one classic model finds two major dimensions—**classical** aesthetics (order, clarity) and **expressive** aesthetics (creativity, originality)—while another influential model finds four facets: **simplicity, diversity, colorfulness, and craftsmanship**. In visual-art preference modeling, researchers have shown that individual judgments can be predicted from mixtures of low-level and high-level features. So the good news is that “taste” is not pure fog. The bad news is that those dimensions are only stable at a fairly coarse level; personal preference still has a strong idiosyncratic residual. ([ScienceDirect][9])

The **mere exposure effect** is the booby trap here. Repeated exposure often increases liking at first, though the effect can saturate or reverse depending on context and repetition. That means Eye Loop is not just *measuring* preference; by showing variants repeatedly, it is also *shaping* it. If you do not control for exposure, your posterior can quietly drift toward “familiar” rather than “true.” ([Nature][10])

The right implication is blunt: your stopping criterion should not be “the model found the exact target.” It should be “the posterior is now tighter than the user’s practical discrimination resolution,” or “additional queries yield little expected information because the remaining differences are below the user’s stable perceptual margin.” Psychophysics is the part of your stack that tells you when you are trying to infer decimals from a human who is answering in vibes. ([PMC][7])

## 3) Artificial immune systems: negative preference has real algorithmic value

Artificial immune systems in computer science traditionally cluster around **negative selection**, **clonal selection**, and **immune-network** models. Negative selection generates detectors, eliminates the ones that match “self,” and retains the rest to detect “non-self.” Computationally, that means random or designed detectors are filtered against an allowed set; the survivors cover forbidden territory. Clonal selection says high-affinity detectors get **copied, mutated, and retained as memory**, which is a near-perfect metaphor for iterative refinement around promising candidates. ([arXiv][11])

Mapped into Eye Loop terms, negative selection becomes a **repulsion model**: don’t just learn what the user likes, learn what they reject, and keep a memory of the rejected regions so you stop wasting queries there. Clonal selection becomes your **acceptance neighborhood search**: clone accepted artifacts, mutate them at different step sizes, and preserve high-affinity lineages as memory cells. Immune-network ideas map to diversity maintenance: keep multiple detector populations so the system does not collapse too early onto one aesthetic basin. ([BSTU Neural Networks Lab][12])

There is also a warning label baked into the AIS literature: the **hole problem**. Negative selection gets hard in high-dimensional spaces, especially near self/non-self boundaries, because detector coverage becomes patchy and computationally expensive. That is directly relevant to Eye Loop. A pure “learn by rejection” strategy will leave blind spots and fail exactly where the boundary is most interesting. So negative memory is valuable, but it cannot be your whole engine. It needs to be paired with a positive utility model and an active query policy. ([Nature][13])

There is a small but real line of work using immune metaphors for art and aesthetic search. Papers on **aesthetic immune systems** and AIS-based art generation explicitly use immune ideas to learn which parts of a search space are worth avoiding or exploring when generating images and biomorphs. It is niche, but it is real prior art for using immune metaphors in creative preference-guided search. ([ResearchGate][14])

## 4) Interactive evolutionary computation: this is your search operator literature

IEC is one of the clearest ancestors of Eye Loop. Systems like **Picbreeder** and **EndlessForms** let humans serve as the fitness function for evolutionary search over images or 3D forms. The most durable lesson is not “humans can evolve art.” It is that **representation is destiny**. Picbreeder’s CPPN/NEAT-style encodings work because they produce regularity, symmetry, and meaningful local variation; small genetic changes often correspond to coherent perceptual changes rather than pixel soup. ([UNM Computer Science][15])

The second lesson is user fatigue, and the literature is almost comically consistent about it. Standard IEC tends to stall because users get tired quickly; one commonly cited limit is on the order of **10–20 generations** for a single-user session. Picbreeder’s workaround was **branching**: users could start from an existing artifact rather than from scratch, effectively inheriting a partially explored lineage. Novelty-assisted IEC went further by letting automation do much of the search while the human periodically steered direction, reducing the number of direct human judgments needed. ([Santa Fe Institute][16])

That suggests three practical rules for Eye Loop. First, cold start should not be random. Seed with **broad, semantically meaningful basis points** or reusable branches. Second, keep the human doing **strategic steering**, not exhaustive evaluation. Third, preserve **diversity and stepping stones**, because aesthetic landscapes are rugged and deceptive; a system that only hill-climbs around current favorites will miss the weird little side road that turns out to be the user’s actual destination. ([UNM Computer Science][15])

## 5) Generative UI: your probes can be interfaces, not just pictures

The most relevant HCI ancestor here is the **Design Galleries** tradition: automatically generate many alternatives, make sure they are perceptually dispersed rather than redundant, and let the user navigate by selection instead of manual parameter tuning. Later work like **Sequential Gallery** made this more formal by using Bayesian optimization to traverse an n-dimensional design space with relatively few user queries. Crowd-powered design-parameter analysis also uses pairwise comparisons to recover preference structure in high-dimensional parameter spaces. This is basically Eye Loop, except the objects are visualizations, layouts, or parameterized designs rather than arbitrary generated images. ([Mitsubishi Electric Labs][17])

This is also where the relation to A/B testing becomes crisp. Classical A/B testing estimates **population-average treatment effects** by exposing many users to a few fixed variants. Eye Loop does almost the inverse: it runs a **within-user adaptive experiment** to infer one user’s latent utility function, changing the variants on the fly. So the closer operational cousins are not fixed A/B tests, but **contextual bandits, dueling bandits, and preferential Bayesian optimization**. ([ScienceDirect][18])

On current LLM-generated UI, the state of the art is promising but not mature enough to be treated like a solved substrate. Benchmarks such as **UI-Bench** and **WebGen-Bench** show rapid progress, but also serious functional brittleness; reported functionality accuracy remains far from “trust this blindly.” Recent work on **Generative and Malleable User Interfaces** argues that direct code generation is a poor foundation for iterative end-user tailoring, and MLLM-based UI judges can help with early evaluation but are not reliable substitutes for humans. So for Eye Loop, the safer pattern is: use LLMs to generate structured UI candidates, but keep the search space anchored in an explicit parameterization or intermediate design model. ([arXiv][19])

## 6) Jungian anima/persona: powerful metaphor, weak canon

This is where the literature gets misty on purpose.

In Jung’s own framework, the **persona** is the social mask, and the **anima** functions as an inward compensatory factor; projection is the process by which unconscious contents are experienced as properties of outer objects. Those ideas are very compatible with your intuition that generated stimuli can reveal the observer’s unarticulated structure. ([Jungian Spiritual Sciences][20])

But as a matter of computational prior art, I did **not** find a broadly accepted mathematical formalization of anima/persona compensation as complement sets, dual spaces, or adversarial representations. I did find adjacent work on computational personality/archetype modeling and some attempts to connect Jungian ideas to predictive-processing or free-energy frameworks, but not a canonical “this is how anima maps into computation” literature. So the honest framing is: this is a **conceptual lens**, not an established technical substrate. ([PubMed][21])

Still, the conceptual mapping is useful. A clean computational interpretation is: persona = the user’s explicit or socially stabilized self-model; anima/shadow = the **latent residual structure** not captured by that model; projection = ambiguous stimuli acting as carriers that cause this residual structure to express itself through preference. In that sense, Eye Loop behaves less like a questionnaire and more like a **projective instrument**. That interpretation also rhymes with modern work reconstructing mental representations from responses to synthetic images built from random deep visual features. I would present this as your theory layer, not your proof layer. ([Jungian Spiritual Sciences][20])

## 7) Multimodal latent spaces: why architecture matters for the loop

Publicly, Google presents **Gemini** as a model family built and trained natively across multiple modalities. By contrast, public OpenAI and Anthropic materials emphasize multimodal capability for GPT-4V and Claude, but disclose much less architecture. Independent surveys of multimodal LLMs describe the most common disclosed pattern as a **vision encoder + adaptor/connector + language model backbone**, not a fully shared representation learned from scratch. ([blog.google][22])

Why should Eye Loop care? Because the better the model’s internal visual-linguistic alignment, the easier it is to do a full generation-comprehension loop: generate a candidate, re-embed it, compare it to the current posterior target, and critique or mutate it coherently. Work like **ImageBind** shows the value of a shared embedding: multiple modalities can inhabit one space and support cross-modal retrieval and composition. I would treat the claim that “shared latent spaces produce better self-evaluation” as plausible but not fully settled in public evidence; what *is* supported is that shared representations improve cross-modal alignment and compositionality, which is exactly what your loop leans on. ([CVF Open Access][23])

## The closest prior systems to Eye Loop

Here are the strongest direct ancestors, from “nearly the same problem” to “same skeleton, different costume.”

**1. Preference-based Image Generation (PbIG, 2020).**
This is probably the nearest direct precedent. It explicitly tries to recover a user’s **mental image** by asking for pairwise comparisons between generated images and updating a preference-based model over a pretrained generator’s latent space. That is Eye Loop with fewer metaphysical accessories and more WACV. The main lesson: pairwise comparison over a generator latent space is viable. ([CVF Open Access][24])

**2. SwipeGANSpace / personalized image generation through swiping (2024).**
This line uses swipe-style interactions to generate images aligned with user preference, often by navigating interpretable directions in GAN latent space. A notable result is that user preferences can **change during interaction**, which matters a lot for your system because you are not just estimating a fixed point; you may be co-constructing it. The lesson: build in preference drift, not just convergence. ([arXiv][25])

**3. GenIR (2025).**
GenIR reframes mental-image retrieval as a multi-round interaction in which the model repeatedly generates images that concretize its current understanding of the user’s target, instead of relying on ambiguous verbal descriptions. This is extremely close to your “the user discovers what they want by selecting” philosophy. The lesson: generated stimuli are not only outputs; they are **communication media for posterior state**. ([arXiv][26])

**4. Preference learning with response time (2025).**
This is the strongest direct support for your latency idea. The work argues and demonstrates that response time can improve preference/reward elicitation when integrated into the model rather than ignored. The lesson: treat RT as first-class evidence. ([arXiv][8])

**5. Picbreeder, EndlessForms, and novelty-assisted IEC.**
These are the practical ancestors for how to keep human-in-the-loop search from becoming a punishment ritual. The lessons are representation quality, branching, novelty search, and letting automation do the boring exploration. ([UNM Computer Science][15])

**6. Design Galleries and Sequential Gallery.**
These are the most relevant ancestors for generative UI/design rather than pure image search. The lesson: your candidates should be **diverse, structured, and query-efficient**, not just plentiful. ([Mitsubishi Electric Labs][17])

**7. Aesthetic immune systems and AIS-based art.**
These are niche but directly relevant to your “negative space” intuition. The lesson: explicit modeling of what not to explore can save human judgments—up to the point where the high-dimensional hole problem bites you. ([ResearchGate][14])

## Where all the threads actually converge

They converge on a single underlying mechanism:

**Eye Loop is an adaptive psychophysical preference search over a generative latent manifold.** The artifacts are queries. The human is a noisy but information-rich measurement device. Reaction time estimates margin. Active learning chooses the next probe. IEC supplies mutation and branching. AIS supplies negative memory. Generative UI gives you structured design spaces. Multimodal alignment lets one model both produce and interpret the probes. Jung gives you the story for *why* ambiguous probes reveal latent structure—but the machinery itself is mathematical, not mystical. ([ResearchGate][1])

That also tells you what literature you were missing or underweighting. The most important adjacent keywords for Eye Loop are probably **active preference learning**, **dueling bandits**, **preferential Bayesian optimization**, **Bayesian adaptive choice experiments**, **pairwise reward modeling**, and **mental-image reconstruction / reverse correlation**. Those are the places where the theory becomes operational instead of poetic. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][27])

## What Eye Loop should steal, shamelessly

First, model the user with a **pairwise preference model plus RT**, not just a binary accept/reject log. Pairwise data fit the strongest existing theory, and RT helps tell you when a choice was easy, conflicted, or nearly indifferent. If you keep accept/reject, anchor it with periodic pairwise comparisons so the threshold does not drift invisibly. ([ICML][28])

Second, use **active query selection** rather than sampling random variations. Choose comparisons near the model’s current uncertainty boundary, but force in diversity so you do not get trapped in one basin. BALD-style information gain, committee disagreement, Fisher-information criteria, or preferential BO are the right family. ([NeurIPS Papers][2])

Third, maintain both a **positive attractor model** and a **negative exclusion model**. The positive model says where the user seems to be heading. The negative model says which neighborhoods are now probably dead. That is the cleanest way to import AIS without getting lost in metaphor. ([Nature][13])

Fourth, use a **coarse-to-fine hierarchy**. Start by learning major axes of taste—simplicity vs richness, orderly vs expressive, sparse vs ornamental, etc.—then refine locally. That is how you buy down the dimensionality curse. ([ScienceDirect][9])

Fifth, control for **mere exposure** and **preference drift**. Repetition changes liking, and interaction can change the target. So your posterior should be stateful, not static: time-decay old evidence, track novelty, and distinguish “preferred because it is closer” from “preferred because I’ve seen it three times already.” ([Nature][10])

Sixth, for generative UI, do not rely on raw text-to-code alone. Use structured intermediate representations, parameterized layouts, or component grammars so mutations are meaningful and comparable. Today’s LLM UI generation is useful, but still too brittle to serve as the only substrate for a precision elicitation loop. ([arXiv][29])

My blunt summary: the Eye Loop is most credible when described as **Bayesian active preference learning with psychophysical observation, evolutionary proposal operators, and multimodal generative probes**. The Jungian language is excellent as philosophy and product mythology; the heavy lifting, though, is being done by information theory, choice modeling, psychophysics, and search. That’s good news. It means your idea is not floating in air. It already has a skeleton.
